Part 1: Setting Up Power

The first step in setting up this lab was to supply our circuits with power. Different circuits for different applications will have different power requirements, but for our purposes, a 9 volt battery should suffice. In fact, we will rarely need more than 3.3V or 5V. In the starter kits we have, we are given a pretty nifty device called a Power Supply Module. This module not only regulates the voltage to both 3.3V and 5V, but also fits onto a standard breadboard and powers the rails that run along the sides. This makes wiring a little cleaner. The Power Supply Module is shown correctly installed in the image below:

The 9V batter is connected to the Power Supply Module by way of a barrel jack that can be seen on the bottom left of the image. On the right side of the module, note that the "+" and "-" icons line up with the red and blue lines on the breadboard, respectively. Finally, notice the white shunts directly to the left of these icons. These shunts allow control over whether the Power Supply Module is supplying 3.3V or 5V to each individual side of the breadboard. Extra Volt

The Extra Volt this week asks us to create a circuit where the button's role is reversed. The LED should light when the button is not pressed, and should turn off when the button is pressed. This circuit demonstrates the logical NOT operation.

The obvious issue is that pressing the button closes that connection in the circuit. The way to overcome this issue is to use this button to create a path of least resistance to ground. This requires the button to be placed in parallel to the Part 2 circuit instead of in series as we did in Part 3. When the button is not pressed, the circuit acts as it did in Part 2 and the LED remains lit. When the button pressed, a path with nearly zero resistance is created. Because the resistance of our 100立 is essentially infinitely times more than the resistance of the new path, almost all of the current is diverted away from that LED and it turns off.
